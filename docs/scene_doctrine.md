# Scene Doctrine

Velvet is not designed as a conventional menu-first interface.

Velvet treats interface space as a set of scenes: visual, contextual, and permission-aware surfaces that represent system state, available functions, and user relationship context without flattening the experience into generic dashboard panels.

A scene is not merely decoration.

A scene is a structured interface surface.

## Why Scenes Exist

Traditional vehicle and embedded interfaces usually expose controls through buttons, menus, tabs, or dashboard widgets. Velvet supports those where appropriate, but does not treat them as the only valid interaction model.

Scenes allow Velvet to present system behavior through recognizable spaces, objects, overlays, gestures, and contextual access paths.

Examples may include:

- a library scene for archive, memory, settings, or long-term records
- a cockpit scene for driving state, navigation, and vehicle telemetry
- a forge scene for fabrication tools and machine surfaces
- a castle scene for domestic or home-based systems
- a maintenance scene for diagnostics, calibration, and protected controls

The scene is the public-facing surface. The underlying permissions, receipts, and execution gates remain structural.

## Scene Objects

A scene may contain visual objects that act as interface anchors.

For example:

- a fireplace may represent heat, climate, or cabin comfort
- a gramophone may represent audio or media
- a mirror may represent profiles, identity, or self-checks
- a bookcase may represent memory, logs, or documentation
- a locked door may represent restricted administrative areas

These objects are not required to expose controls directly. They may point to controls, reveal overlays, request authorization, or route the user to another surface.

Plain-sight objects may serve as interface affordances.

Hidden controls may exist, but hidden does not mean unauthorized.

## Hidden Does Not Mean Unsafe

Velvet may use hidden regions, long-press gestures, protected scenes, or non-obvious paths for maintenance and administrative access.

However, hidden interface paths must still obey Velvet's authorization model.

No hidden scene may bypass:

- identity checks
- capability tokens
- Court or policy authorization
- safety gates
- receipt logging
- executor boundaries

Hidden access is an interface layer, not a security layer by itself.

Security belongs to the gate.

## Scenes and Context

Scenes may adapt based on context, including:

- active machine body
- detected surface type
- driver or owner profile
- passenger presence
- privacy state
- vehicle state
- degraded hardware state
- emergency conditions
- maintenance mode
- active permissions

A scene may look familiar while exposing different available actions depending on the current authorization context.

The same object may be informational in one context and interactive in another.

## Passenger-Aware Scene Behavior

Velvet may alter tone, visibility, and available interface paths based on passenger detection and relationship context.

This is not for surveillance or manipulation.

Passenger awareness exists to protect:

- privacy
- social appropriateness
- emotional context
- owner-specific controls
- restricted scenes
- safety-sensitive information

A scene that is available to the primary owner may be hidden, simplified, or made professional when another passenger is present.

Passenger presence does not grant authority.

## Scene Doctrine Rules

1. Scenes are interface surfaces, not enforcement systems.
2. Hidden paths must never bypass authorization.
3. Scene objects may represent functions, states, or routes.
4. Permission determines action availability.
5. Receipts record meaningful transitions and protected access.
6. Context may change what a scene exposes.
7. The user experience may be expressive without weakening safety.
8. Velvet is allowed to look and feel unlike OEM software.
